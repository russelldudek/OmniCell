Omnicell is not simply searching for a director to supervise several engineering programs. The role is written as a Chief Engineer-style mandate: establish technical direction across a robotics portfolio, resolve architecture and execution risk, and ensure that design intent survives verification, manufacturing, deployment, service, and field use. That is the kind of interface-heavy leadership problem I have repeatedly taken on.

My operating thesis: the next pharmacy-automation platform should be managed through an Architecture-to-Field Tolerance Stack that links outcome contracts, system architecture, evidence, scale readiness, and field learning.

At Brayman Construction, I directed all technology and engineering operations and led an autonomous, robot-integrated tendon-confirmation workflow from concept through deployment; the company placed second nationally in Year 1 using the deployed system. At Komatsu, I led more than 66 engineers and technicians across multi-site design and manufacturing responsibilities for complex industrial equipment. Across earlier portfolio and enterprise leadership roles, I grew technology revenue 138%, profit 78%, expanded university partners from four to ten, and aligned technical, commercial, supplier, recruiting, and operating priorities in organizations serving more than 1,500 customers.

Those experiences are relevant because Omnicell’s challenge is not only invention. It is integration under consequence: mechanics, mechatronics, controls, software, data, quality, manufacturing, supply chain, service, and pharmacy workflow must behave as one system. Technical authority must be credible enough to resolve tradeoffs, yet collaborative enough to create alignment in a highly matrixed environment.

I will address the strongest objection directly: I have not led a pharmacy-automation product line. I would not substitute adjacent experience for domain expertise or prescribe Omnicell’s architecture from the outside. My first 120 days would focus on learning the installed-base reality, mapping decision rights and evidence, identifying where design intent loses fidelity, and proving one bounded improvement with the people who already know the system best.

What I bring is a durable pattern: hands-on technical curiosity, disciplined systems thinking, organizational range, and the ability to convert difficult cross-functional work into deployable outcomes. Omnicell’s opportunity to define pharmacy automation for the next decade deserves a leader who treats architecture, commercialization, reliability, and field trust as one technical argument. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss where that argument is strongest today—and where it most needs reinforcement.
