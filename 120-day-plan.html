Earn context. Clarify the technical contract. Prove the mechanism.

A 120-day entry plan designed for a Chief Engineer-style role in a matrixed product organization.

Entry principles
Listen before prescribing.
Pharmacy, nursing, quality, service, and customer context are engineering inputs.
Make current strengths visible.
The goal is not to replace working mechanisms, but to understand and reinforce them.
Use live decisions.
Prove the operating model on real work rather than creating a parallel bureaucracy.
120-day outcomes
OutcomeEvidence by day 120
Portfolio architecture clarityCommon versus product-specific boundaries, interface ownership, and priority decisions are explicit.
Technical authorityDecision rights, escalation criteria, evidence expectations, and review cadence are understood.
Concept-to-scale continuityClinical intent, verification, industrialization, installation, service, and field learning appear in the same decision record.
Reliability learningOne recurring field pattern has a normalized signal, causal hypothesis, containment, and architecture/verification disposition.
Next-generation executionOne concept has passed an Architecture-to-Field Integrity Review with explicit next decisions and owners.
Portfolio roadmapNext-quarter technical priorities balance innovation, reliability, platform leverage, and commercialization burden.
Guardrails

No unsupported maturity scores. No assumption that current processes are broken. No architecture theater detached from products. No attempt to centralize every decision. The entry plan is a discovery hypothesis to refine with Omnicell leadership.

Days 0–60 | Build the shared technical picture

Days 0–15 | Listen at the interfaces

  • Meet Product, robotics/mechatronics, software, systems, Quality, Regulatory, Manufacturing, Supply Chain, Service, Customer Success, clinical leadership, and key portfolio executives.
  • Observe product use and service reality where access permits: pharmacy, nursing, technicians, installations, escalation, and maintenance.
  • Ask what must not be broken, which tradeoffs are recurrent, and where decisions wait for clarity.

Evidence: stakeholder topology, decision-rights hypotheses, clinical/workflow questions, protected strengths.

Days 16–30 | Trace the portfolio system

  • Map product/platform architecture at the level appropriate for portfolio decisions: common elements, variants, interfaces, configuration, and lifecycle.
  • Review recent concept, verification, launch, reliability, supplier-change, installation, and field-learning examples.
  • Identify where product performance, manufacturability, serviceability, quality, or schedule compete.

Evidence: current-state architecture map, field-to-design signal path, decision and evidence inventory.

Days 31–45 | Define the technical contract

  • Propose architecture principles and explicit boundaries for common platform versus product-specific optimization.
  • Define decision classes: local team, cross-functional review, technical-authority resolution, executive escalation.
  • Agree on minimum evidence by decision type and release scope.

Evidence: draft architecture principles, authority matrix, tradeoff-record schema.

Days 46–60 | Establish cadence and scorecard

  • Integrate with existing forums rather than inventing a parallel governance layer.
  • Define a balanced scorecard spanning clinical/workflow, system performance, architecture, reliability, economics, and learning speed.
  • Select two live decisions for proof: one next-generation concept and one installed-base reliability pattern.

Evidence: review cadence, metric definitions, two proof cases with named owners.

Days 61–120 | Prove and institutionalize

Days 61–75 | Review the next-generation concept

  • Run the Architecture-to-Field Integrity Review with Product, Engineering, Quality, Manufacturing, Supply Chain, and Service.
  • Clarify clinical outcome, architecture boundary, novelty, failure modes, evidence, build/service burden, and learning plan.
  • Resolve, bound, hold, or escalate the next decision.

Deliverable: reviewed concept, evidence gaps, decision owner, release posture, downstream commitments.

Days 76–90 | Close a reliability-learning loop

  • Normalize a recurring field signal and distinguish symptom, service variation, product defect, interface weakness, and architecture debt.
  • Connect containment, reproduction, root cause, corrective action, verification, and recurrence test.
  • Translate the lesson into an architecture principle, design standard, or verification case where supported.

Deliverable: one field-to-architecture learning record and measurable closure path.

Days 91–105 | Align portfolio priorities

  • Review roadmap choices through innovation value, platform leverage, reliability burden, technical debt, supply risk, and commercialization readiness.
  • Identify decisions that need executive resolution and those teams can own locally.
  • Make capacity and sequence implications visible.

Deliverable: portfolio technical-priority map and executive decision agenda.

Days 106–120 | Lock the next operating cycle

  • Refine the review mechanism from user feedback; remove low-value steps.
  • Set next-quarter architecture, concept, reliability, and industrialization priorities with named owners.
  • Publish decision rules, evidence expectations, and learning cadence in the systems teams already use.

Deliverable: next-quarter roadmap, recurring cadence, scorecard definitions, and coaching priorities.

Day-120 review questions
  • Are important technical decisions faster or merely more documented?
  • Can teams explain why a decision was made and what evidence would change it?
  • Has one field signal changed product or verification learning?
  • Do Product, Engineering, Quality, Manufacturing, and Service see the same release posture?
