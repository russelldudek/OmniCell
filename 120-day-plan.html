Russell Dudek · Candidate entry plan

First 120 days: learn the system before changing the system

The purpose of the first 120 days is not to arrive with a preselected architecture. It is to build an evidence-backed view of the portfolio, clarify technical decision rights, identify where design intent loses fidelity, and prove one bounded operating improvement with the teams who own the work.

0–30

Listen, observe, and map the field reality

Build credibility through technical immersion and tactical empathy. Learn how pharmacy workflow, patient-safety expectations, product architecture, quality systems, manufacturing, deployment, and service actually interact.

  • Conduct structured listening sessions across Engineering, Product, Quality, Manufacturing, Supply Chain, Service, Clinical/workflow experts, and executive leadership.
  • Observe design reviews, defect reviews, production flow, integration test, installation preparation, and service escalation.
  • Trace two recent product decisions from market need through field consequence.
  • Create an initial stakeholder and decision-rights map.
  • Inventory current architecture views, requirements, risk records, verification evidence, configuration controls, and field-signal channels.
  • Identify language mismatches that conceal cross-functional disagreement.
Day-30 evidence: validated system map · decision-rights baseline · top learning questions · no premature reorganization

Questions this phase must answer

System truth

  • Which interfaces fail repeatedly?
  • Which assumptions are least visible?
  • Where is observability weak?
  • Which field signals arrive too late?

Organization truth

  • Who owns portfolio decisions?
  • Where does escalation stall?
  • Which functions carry hidden burden?
  • Where has trust been taxed?

Days 31–75

Create a shared architecture and evidence view

31–45

Frame the portfolio-level technical mandate

Convert the first month’s observations into a reviewable problem statement rather than a private diagnosis.

  • Draft the Architecture-to-Field Tolerance Stack using Omnicell’s actual language, artifacts, and governance.
  • Separate documented facts, observed patterns, unresolved questions, and hypotheses.
  • Identify portfolio-level boundaries, reusable capabilities, and program-specific exceptions.
  • Map the strongest technical contradictions and the functions carrying each side of the tradeoff.
Gate: leadership agrees on the problem framing before solution design begins.
46–60

Align technical decision rights and evidence expectations

Reduce decision latency without centralizing every technical choice.

  • Define which decisions belong to platform architecture, programs, functional experts, quality, manufacturing, service, and executives.
  • Classify decisions by reversibility, installed-base consequence, supplier/tooling commitment, and patient/workflow risk.
  • Create a lightweight technical decision record with assumptions, evidence, dissent, owner, expiration, and field-learning plan.
  • Agree on escalation criteria for unresolved cross-functional risk.
Gate: teams can predict where a decision belongs and what evidence it requires.
61–75

Select one bounded integrity-review pilot

Choose a live decision or recurring interface problem where improved visibility can create value without destabilizing delivery.

  • Prioritize a problem with executive relevance, measurable friction, willing owners, and bounded scope.
  • Baseline decision time, rework, verification ambiguity, service burden, or recurrence.
  • Run the Architecture-to-Field Integrity Review with the existing team.
  • Capture what the mechanism clarifies—and where it creates unnecessary process.
Day-75 evidence: agreed decision taxonomy · pilot baseline · visible ownership · protected delivery commitments

Change-burden guardrails

No shadow system

Adapt existing artifacts before introducing parallel repositories or ceremonies.

No governance theater

Every new review must close a decision, expose risk, or retire work.

No expert displacement

Architecture authority integrates domain expertise; it does not overwrite it.

Days 76–120

Prove value and establish the portfolio rhythm

76–95

Complete the pilot and measure the difference

Use the pilot to test whether the proposed mechanism improves technical clarity and execution—not whether teams can comply with a new template.

  • Resolve or materially advance the pilot decision with documented evidence and ownership.
  • Measure change in decision latency, ambiguity, uncovered interfaces, rework risk, or downstream burden.
  • Interview participants separately to identify trust gains, friction, and blind spots.
  • Decide what to retain, simplify, or discard.
Gate: evidence supports continuation; the mechanism is not scaled by enthusiasm alone.
96–110

Build the portfolio technical agenda

Translate validated learning into a small number of portfolio-level priorities connected to business and field outcomes.

  • Identify the highest-leverage architecture, evidence, scale, and field-learning priorities.
  • Connect each priority to customer/workflow value, lifecycle economics, risk, and accountable owners.
  • Expose dependencies across current products, next-generation programs, manufacturing, service, and installed base.
  • Recommend sequencing that protects current delivery while creating future platform leverage.
Gate: each priority has an outcome, owner, evidence path, and explicit displaced work.
111–120

Institutionalize the minimum effective operating rhythm

Create a durable cadence for portfolio architecture and cross-functional technical decisions.

  • Establish a concise portfolio architecture review focused on consequential decisions and interfaces.
  • Integrate field and service signals into the architecture backlog with clear triage and closure rules.
  • Publish decision rights, escalation paths, and a small technical-health scorecard.
  • Present the first-year technical agenda, organizational support needs, and unresolved discovery questions.
